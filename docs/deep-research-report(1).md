## Current Operational Status (FACTS)

- **Hormuz transit:** *Effectively closed.* Commercial vessel counts have collapsed (e.g. AIS shows ~80–95% drop in traffic【8†L240-L249】【47†L159-L168】). Shipping lanes remain legally open, but *nearly all vessels are avoiding transit*【45†L200-L204】【47†L151-L160】.  
- **Incident rate (7d):** *Multiple attacks.* At least 5 significant vessel incidents (3–4 tankers struck on 1–2 Mar【43†L189-L197】【45†L250-L253】, plus the Stena Imperative in port) have occurred in the past week. UKMTO reports 3 strikes in a 24h span on 2 Mar【3†L226-L233】.  No known friendly-fire incidents reported.  
- **Electronic interference:** *Widespread.* AIS/GPS/communications disruptions are widely reported in the Gulf region【40†L10-L18】【28†L373-L378】. Mariners are alerted to intermittent AIS blackouts and VHF spoofing.  
- **Insurance/war-risk:** *Withdrawn.* Major P&I clubs have cancelled coverage for the Gulf/Iran waters【45†L259-L267】, war-risk premiums have spiked (~0.2%→1% ship value【45†L276-L283】), and some owners temporarily refuse transit【47†L159-L168】.  Contracts note de facto risk zone.  
- **Red Sea / Bab-el-Mandeb:** *High risk.* Yemen’s Houthis have signaled resumption of missile/drone attacks in the Red Sea【49†L21-L28】. EU NAVFOR’s ASPIDES is on heightened alert.  Some vessels are again rerouting (rates still elevated). A “dual-chokepoint” threat is emerging if Horn and Red Sea risks coincide【49†L19-L28】.  Incident rate north of Red Sea remains moderate for now.

## Buffers & Substitution

- **Strategic Reserves:** Most Asian importers have substantial crude/fuel stockpiles.  Japan’s emergency reserves cover ~254 days【66†L249-L258】, South Korea ~208 days【66†L269-L274】, China ~180 days (∼900 MMbbl)【66†L231-L236】, and India ~20–25 days of supply (though “coverage 74 days” including all stocks【64†L337-L346】【66†L210-L218】).  Pakistan reports ~28 days of fuel stocks【57†L76-L84】, and the Philippines claims ~50–60 days【62†L47-L50】.  Global IEA members typically hold 2–3 months of crude reserves (not shown).  
- **Alternate pipelines/ports:** Gulf exporters are exploiting built-for-this routes: Saudi Aramco’s East-West pipeline (Abqaiq–Yanbu) has **~5 mbpd capacity**【52†L238-L244】 (briefly handled 7 mbpd in 2019) and can deliver Saudi oil to the Red Sea.  The UAE’s Habshan–Fujairah (ADCOP) pipeline moves **1.5 mbpd** of Abu Dhabi crude to Oman Sea【52†L269-L277】.  However, **current loading at Yanbu is <1.5 mbpd**【52†L239-L244】, far below need (Saudi + UAE exports ~7 mbpd originally). Iraq has no equivalent Gulf-to-Red Sea bypass of meaningful capacity.  
- **Rerouting effects:** Tankers detouring via the Cape of Good Hope dramatically increase voyage length.  Early signs: VLCC freight rates have **more than doubled** to Asia (e.g. ~$28 M vs ~$12 M)【52†L260-L268】.  More ships are pooling at alternate terminals (e.g. Jeddah/Yanbu, Fujairah) awaiting assignment.  *Ton-mile demand* has soared, meaning fewer voyages per ship-year.  Overall tanker availability tightens (VLCC fixes surge) and oil shipping costs hit multi-year highs【45†L286-L289】【52†L260-L268】.  
- **What cannot be substituted:**  **About 18–20 mbpd** of crude normally transits Hormuz; pipelines can handle at most ~6–7 mbpd to the Red Sea【52†L238-L244】【52†L269-L277】.  No route exists for, e.g., Saudi crude to Asia apart from pipe or Suez transit.  **Petroleum products** (refined fuels) mostly flow by tanker and cannot bypass via pipelines, so only stockpiles or reduced consumption can substitute.  Deep-sea tanker availability and global refinery utilization cannot immediately replace a multi-week closure of this key chokepoint.

## Asia Importer Impact (30/60/90 days)

Major Asian economies are heavily exposed to Gulf imports (roughly 50–95% of crude from Mideast【66†L206-L214】【66†L243-L251】).  Key country summaries:

- **Pakistan:** ~100% of its crude/petroleum comes via Hormuz【57†L62-L70】.  Fuel inventories are ~28 days of demand【57†L76-L84】, above legal minimum.  With a month’s supply on hand, near-term shortages are unlikely unless the crisis extends much beyond April.  Failure mode: if >1 month, **fuel shortages and rationing** risk (particularly diesel); markets already spiking.  Pakistan is exploring alternate suppliers (Iran, Russia, Venezuela【57†L106-L110】).  Government levers: daily stock monitoring, import diversions from Iran/Venezuela, possible price subsidies or demand cuts (petrol rationing or higher LPG imports).

- **India:** ~55% of crude imports from Mideast (2.74 mbd)【66†L206-L214】. India is most vulnerable in Asia due to this thin effective buffer【55†L214-L223】【64†L379-L387】.  Near-term failure: **refinery slowdowns or product shortages** if disruption >3–4 weeks; risk of rolling blackouts if diesel for power/transport runs low.  Price spikes (already occurring) could trigger inflation.  Levers: tapping SPRs (filling at Mangalore/Visakhapatnam), diversifying imports (more U.S., Africa, Russia【64†L377-L385】), releasing strategic inventories, fuel subsidies/demand restraint, diplomatic talks to restore flows.

- **Thailand:** >50% of crude from Gulf (SPG: “more than half”【60†L1-L4】) and holds ~60 days of oil stocks【60†L1-L4】.  With ~2 months’ supply and over 90% refined fuel coverage by reserves, Thailand is relatively cushioned in the short run.  Failure mode: thermal power increases (utilities shifting from LNG to coal/hydro) are planned to save fuel, but gasoline/diesel prices will rise.  Government already suspended fuel exports to save stocks【59†L1453-L1461】 and is monitoring supply.  Levers: mobilize SPRs, impose temporary consumption cuts (e.g. lower refinery runs), and use fuel subsidies to shield consumers if needed.

- **China:** ~50% of oil imports from Mideast【66†L226-L236】.  Boasts very large reserves (~900 MMbbl, ≈3 months)【66†L231-L236】.  China has ample buffer and can draw on global stockpiles (large spot inventories in Asia).  It also has minor pipelines (Russia, Myanmar) but these are limited. - **Japan:** ~95% of oil from Mideast (70% via Hormuz)【66†L243-L251】.  Holds **~254 days** of oil in stockpiles【66†L249-L258】, among the world’s largest buffers.  Japan also has strategic LNG inventories (~3 weeks of LNG【66†L252-L260】).  Near-term impact will be mostly price-driven; physically, refineries can run on reserves. - **South Korea:** ~70% of oil from Mideast【66†L263-L272】.  Total reserves ~195 MMbbl (100 MM govt + 95 MM commercial)【66†L269-L274】, covering ~208 days consumption.  Government announced seeking alternate imports if needed【66†L273-L279】.  Failure mode: minimal risk of immediate shortage due to large stocks; focus will be on LNG (20% from ME) and refining outputs. - **Philippines:** Essentially 100% import-reliant.  President Marcos assured ~50–60 days of oil in reserve【62†L47-L50】.  Failure mode: rising petrol/gas prices and inflation; potential social unrest if shortages occur after ~2 months.  Levers: already budgeting fuel subsidies【62†L72-L80】, encouraging stockpiling of refined products, and monitoring supply chains.

## Scenario Tree & Probabilities (INTERPRETATION)

We consider three broad 90-day scenarios (probabilities sum to ~100%):

- **1. De-Escalation / Stabilization (20–30%)**: Conflict diffusion. Iran halts attacks in exchange for diplomatic concessions; risk zone contractually defined (e.g. escorted convoys). *Leads:* War-risk cover gradually reinstated; AIS traffic rises; oil prices fall back from current spike.  
  - **Must be true:** Iran or proxies cease attacks; US/Israel curb strikes; successful diplomacy (e.g. new negotiations).  
  - **Indicators:** Formal ceasefire talk, high-level talks mediated (GCC/UN), US/Central Command statements lowering alert, war-risk premium easing.  
  - **Resolution condition:** Ships transit under escort or agreed safe corridors; normal insurance cover resumes; tanker counts approach normal levels (e.g. >70% of baseline).

- **2. Prolonged “Effective Closure” (50–60%)**: Status quo persists. Attacks continue sporadically; insurers keep pulling back; shippers refuse transit. *Leads:* SoH remains effectively closed. Asia relies on buffers and costly reroutes.  
  - **Must be true:** Iran continues restricting passage (via threats/attacks) but avoids full war (no major counterstrikes by US); shipping insurers remain withdrawn.  
  - **Indicators:** Continued or rising war-risk premiums; new UKMTO/MARAD alerts; constant AIS counts near zero; no major diplomacy wins; repeated small-scale attacks (projectile hits or drones).  
  - **Resolution condition:** A demonstrable drop in incidents and warnings; either negotiated agreements or US-led security umbrella; war-risk cover reinstated.

- **3. Escalation / Wider Conflict (20–30%)**: Conflict intensifies or expands. Iran or proxies strike Gulf oil facilities or U.S. regional bases; U.S./allies ramp up military response. *Leads:* Full-scale disruption, potentially major supply shock (longer blockade or retaliation).  
  - **Must be true:** New Iranian strikes (e.g. missile barrage on Gulf oilfields, attacks on US ships/bases); U.S. engages more forcefully (troop/legal escalations); Houthi intensify Red Sea campaign.  
  - **Indicators:** Significant new military moves (e.g. Iranian missile launches visible on OT models, US forces in high-def readiness, Houthi claiming attacks), oil price surges beyond current levels, global strategic stocks tapped by governments.  
  - **Resolution condition:** End of operations both ways – e.g. Iran stops, US stands down – and establishment of new status quo via ceasefire or victory; otherwise, scenario becomes de-escalation only if active hostilities truly end.

*Probabilities (estimates):* We assign most weight to **stalemate (Effective Closure)** as current conditions seem entrenched; de-escalation is possible but requires mutual restraint; full-blown war is significant tail risk if miscalculation occurs.

## Definitions

- **“Legally open” vs “Effectively closed”:** Legally open means no formal blockade or UN-acknowledged closure exists; vessels have right of passage【40†L19-L25】. *Effectively closed* means that in practice ships avoid the route due to security/insurance risk (as is happening now)【45†L200-L204】【47†L151-L160】. In other words, international law still allows transit, but commercial shipping has *de facto* halted.

- **“Resolution” (operational):** A stable situation where routine shipping resumes safely.  Key signs are normal AIS flows, reinstated insurance cover, and no new attacks. Operationally, Hormuz would be as usable as before the crisis (even if diplomatic issues remain). 

- **“Rewind”:** A reversal of risk.  Measurable improvements that push the clock backward include: clear waters (no incidents), restoration of war-risk insurance, normalized carrier shipping levels, and lower freight/oil prices. For example, a drop in war premiums and convoy announcements would signify a retreat from the current risk.

## What we know

- The Strait of Hormuz is **functionally shut** for most commerce【45†L200-L204】【47†L151-L160】.  All data (AIS counts, P&I notices) confirm an 80–95% traffic collapse.  
- Several tankers and one offshore terminal have already been hit (total at least 5 incidents, multiple casualties)【43†L189-L197】【45†L250-L257】.  Iran has effectively threatened any transit【45†L221-L224】.  
- Major insurers and governments view the region as high-risk.  War-risk cover has been canceled【45†L259-L267】, and U.S. is moving to escort convoys【22†L249-L258】, indicating official concern.  
- Asian buyers hold significant crude stockpiles (Japan/SK/China have multi-month buffers【66†L243-L251】【66†L269-L274】).  Imports from Gulf range 50–95%, so impact is broad.  
- Alternate routes exist (Yanbu, Fujairah pipelines【52†L238-L244】【52†L269-L277】) but are far below needed capacity.  Global flows must be drastically rerouted, raising tanker demand and freight.  

## What we don’t know

- **Duration:** How long Iran (or its proxies) will maintain attacks/closure is unclear.  Will attacks persist daily, or is a short flare?  
- **Actors’ intent:** It’s uncertain whether Iran’s leaders seek a limited deterrence or aim to coerce a far-reaching embargo.  Similarly, U.S./allies’ willingness to re-escalate (militarily or via diplomacy) is unknown.  
- **Houthi behavior:** The interplay with Red Sea risks – will Houthis expand attacks to pressure shipping?  The extent of their follow-through on renewed threats is uncertain.  
- **Market response:** Whether oil consumers/states will release strategic reserves (e.g. via IEA) or re-open Iraqi/Turkish pipelines is not yet decided.  Price and supply reactions may change actual shortage conditions.  

## What would change the probabilities fastest

- **Major military moves:** A large strike (e.g. Iranian missile salvo on Gulf oilfields or Houthi barrage) or a robust escort deployment would dramatically shift the balance to *Escalation* or *Stabilization*, respectively.  
- **Diplomatic breakthroughs:** A formal ceasefire or negotiation (e.g. UN-mediated talks) could pivot toward *De-Escalation*.  Conversely, breakdown in talks or legal blockades (even symbolic) would entrench *Closure*.  
- **Insurance reinstatement:** If insurers start offering normal cover again, it implies perceived risk has receded (de-escalation). Continued cancellations reinforce the critical/closure scenario.  
- **Transit metrics:** Rapid rise in AIS transits (hundreds per day) would signal effective reopening. Sustained zero traffic keeps the closure alarm high.
